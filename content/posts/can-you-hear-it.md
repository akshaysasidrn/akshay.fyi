I've been in my introspective pocket lately. To alter my perspective to view things in a new light and rekindle the spark for my craft. What I'm faced with is an identity crisis of sorts, on being [a poet with no verse](/posts/a-poet-with-no-verse/).

One can argue that is a silly predicament, one which is self-inflicted, and quite rightly so. But the way my psyche works, I need some sort of creative or imaginative flair to everyday mundane things I'd be doing or else it will end up sucking the soul out of me.

---

The art of expressing our intention through code is now commoditised. You can have AI spit out _N_ variations of your intention on demand. The traditional way of doing it by your hand now seems meaningless. When you think about it in context of the real world, it was only the end result that really mattered. The software runtime. It's un/fortunate(?) that the part of the process that I fell in love with, to get to that runtime, has evolved. Which necessitates me to evolve as well.

As an engineer, it is my job to always find the best tools at my disposal and use them to get to the end result quickly and reliably. This has now led me on a journey of trying these tools without any inhibition. And in the process of doing so, I think I can see a new light at the end of the tunnel.

> Writing computer software is one of the purest creative activities in the history of the human race. Programmers aren't bound by practical limitations such as the laws of physics; we can create exciting virtual worlds with behaviors that could never exist in the real world. 
>
> All programming requires is a creative mind and the ability to organize your thoughts. If you can visualize a system, you can probably implement it in a computer program. 
> - from: **"A Philosophy of Software Design"** - John K. Ousterhout 

That visualization, that intent, that's still yours. What's changed is that the implementation is now delegated, and the effort of creation has reduced significantly if you open yourself up to these tools. How best to leverage them is, IMHO, the great up-skilling we need.

And this is where I admit that I have not been writing (or rather, I've been forcing myself not to write) any code since January of this year. But that doesn't mean I haven't been programming. I still have the intent and the visualization of what needs to be done. But rather than coding it myself, I let the AI do it for me. My programming brain has upshifted to the problem space of how best to program these AI tools to create programs the way I intend.

Of course, there is nothing stopping you from saying things like:

*"Build me X. Make no mistakes!"* (╯°□°）╯︵ ┻━┻

Which is no different from copy-pasting from Stack Overflow not so long ago. You get something that works until it doesn't. It'll never get you far in your journey to being a skilled software craftsman.

The way I see it, this is another programming paradigm we need to warm ourselves to. One which is bleeding edge, and therefore quite hard to get up to speed with. Daunting, even, as there's a lot of theory to read, patterns that keep emerging, FOMO-inducing Twitter/X chatter, and an entire SDLC being disrupted as we know it.

What worked amidst all this chaos for me was picking up one tool and building an intuition for it. Try different AI tools out there for your programming work and see what clicks for you. Claude Code, Antigravity, Copilot, Codex, whatever. For me it was Claude Code. The moment it was available in the terminal, it slotted into my head like any other terminal program, ready to be composed alongside the battle-tested tools already there. The UX is [UNIX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unix_philosophy).

```bash
echo "I scrolled socials, took a nap & procrastinated at work today" |
claude -p "Convert this to Linkedinese"
```
And you get:
```
Today I practiced mindful digital consumption, 
engaged in strategic cognitive recovery, and embraced
asynchronous priority realignment in the workplace.

Growth isn't always visible. Sometimes it's internal.

Agree?
```

 This implies that every program at your terminal is a candidate for an AI wrapper. Which you can pipe and compose however you like. Slap an API over it and your compositions are _AI wrapped_. 

That's the principle, simplified. Every product in this space builds on it, just at a higher scale, with fancier packaging. The AI wrapper gold rush. But you don't need to wait for that. You've got a terminal full of composable programs right now. You just need to figure out how they fit into your workflow. Get a little creative with it. 

Once you start actually using it though, you'll notice it's a bit flaky. You won't always get consistent results. So you start building a corrective loop, an evaluation step where the AI can verify and fix its own output. And then it clicks: *this* is what everyone means when they say "AI accelerated". Not just throwing AI at a problem, but having a reliable loop around it that gets you to deterministic-enough output, consistently.

Getting that loop right requires you to visualize the system clearly: what goes in, what should come out, and where it can go wrong. That's just **programming**. Except once the [loop](https://martinfowler.com/articles/exploring-gen-ai/humans-and-agents.html) is working, you're operating at a scale that wasn't possible before. This then leaves you with more headspace to be able to do more. You can use this headspace to think more on your architecture, scrutinize and refine your SDLC practices, upskill yourself on things you wanted to. You get the drift, right?

Another side-effect I noticed: the age-old software engineering practices that were often quietly skipped in the name of shipping quickly, but never acknowledged unless everything [caught on 🔥](https://www.workchronicles.com/p/comic-prevention-vs-cure) - like tests, clear interfaces, well-defined contracts, documentation etc have suddenly become all the more important. AI requires them to have these self-corrective loops towards autonomy. Oh, how the tables have turned!? The product and engineering have to be in alignment now. The craftsman in me is happy. ᕕ( ᐛ )ᕗ

---

Some time ago, my buddy had asked me whether I'd switch careers if something else paid the same. Surprisingly, I didn't have to think long. I said I'd switch in a heartbeat, as long as my creative juices kept flowing. Because the ability to create something expressively is what I fell in love with. The reason work always felt like play. And now the question is whether AI can keep those juices flowing.

I think the answer is a *yes*. I might not be expressing my intent through a programming language the way I aspired to. The way programmers showcased their skill till now. But I still get to express it, at a higher level, at a higher scale with the additional headspace this delegation provides.

And as for identity, I think the poet has to put down his pen for now. To try a different role. A composer, perhaps. One who can envision a symphony rather than play one instrument. Who can call on skilled musicians and orchestrate them into something greater. The symphony they play though, that's mine to compose.

The future doesn't seem bleak. Rather it's exciting, as software is going to explode with AI. But best to tune out from the general *"programming is dead"* hot takes, the infinite slop of prompts, how-to AI posts, hot new engineering papers - build an intuition for using the available tools at your hand instead. 

Because theory will take you only so far. For the notes that make a symphony are all around you. Visualize the system.
